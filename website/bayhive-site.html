The Problem

The data exists.
The view doesn't.

Pollinator conservation in northeastern Wisconsin is fragmented across agencies, organizations, and data systems.

The Green Bay Conservation Corps manages a regional corridor. The Wisconsin DNR oversees state natural areas and managed lands. Federal datasets track protected areas and bee distributions. Community scientists log tens of thousands of sightings annually — but no unified tool brings these sources together at the local scale. Without a shared spatial view, decision-making about where to invest restoration effort is more intuition than evidence.

◎
Neglected habitat sites

Sites that receive no documented pollinator activity may be unknowingly overlooked, with no systematic way to identify them.

◎
Unflagged hazard proximity

PFAS contamination sites near sensitive habitat are not systematically cross-referenced with corridor boundaries or waystations.

◎
Invisible restoration candidates

High-sighting parcels with no formal habitat designation — the best restoration candidates — remain invisible without a unified spatial view.

The Tool

One map.
All the data.

Bay Hive integrates more than a dozen authoritative public datasets into a continuously refreshed map centered on a 15-kilometer radius from Green Bay city center. Observation data refreshes hourly; area layers daily. No GIS software, no installation, no paid data subscriptions. It runs with a single command: node serve.js

01

Multi-source sightings

iNaturalist (historical observations back to 2010 — up to 2,000 per calendar year), GBIF records (up to 600), eBird, and Wikimedia Commons photos — filterable by research grade, native species, and proximity to habitat.

02

Conservation layers

USGS PAD-US v3.0, WI DNR State Natural Areas, DNR Managed Lands, GBCC Pollinator Corridor, and Habitat Treatment records in one view.

03

Bee distribution analysis

FWS dataset across all 6 bee families, a species richness heatmap, and an imperiled species layer covering NatureServe G1–G3 and IUCN VU+.

04

Land cover & cropland

NLCD 2021 (16 toggleable classes), USDA Cropland Data Layer with bee-crop fringe heatmap, and WI DNR Urban Tree Canopy (2013, 2020, 2022).

05

Hazard overlay

WI DNR PFAS contamination sites with automated 1 km proximity alerts to habitat nodes and Monarch Watch Waystations.

06

Trend analysis & export

Year-over-year sighting charts once two or more observation snapshots are collected. Full plain-text intelligence report export at any time.

07

Expansion opportunities

0–100 scored candidate sites for new pollinator habitat where ecological conditions are favorable but no registered program exists within 800 m. Six-factor model: native plant records, nesting suitability, corridor proximity, PFAS-free environment, pesticide pressure, and pollinator activity. Three recommendation types guide who to contact.

08

Problem area diagnostics

Seven automated problem conditions checked against every Corridor site in real time: PFAS proximity, pollinator absence, isolation, weak connections, poor nesting habitat, excessive shade, and high pesticide pressure. Updated whenever data refreshes.

09

InVEST pollinator index

Lonsdorf et al. (2009) foraging model computing a continuous relative pollinator abundance index across the study area. Three bee guilds (0.3 / 0.7 / 1.5 km range) with NLCD 2021 biophysical inputs. Shows where pollinators are most likely supported by the surrounding landscape — independent of observation records.

Who It Serves

Built for the people
doing the work.

Bay Hive is designed for a range of users across the conservation and land management ecosystem — from corridor coordinators and agency biologists to community scientists and municipal planners identifying restoration parcels.

⬡

Habitat Coordinators

GBCC staff monitoring corridor health, identifying connectivity gaps, and targeting restoration outreach with evidence rather than intuition.

⬡

Agency Biologists

WI DNR staff reviewing State Natural Area coverage, PFAS proximity alerts, and cross-referencing managed lands with pollinator pressure data.

⬡

Community Scientists

iNaturalist and GBIF contributors who want to see their observations in the context of regional conservation work and corridor health.

⬡

Municipal Planners

City and county parks staff using opportunity zone alerts to identify publicly owned parcels that could anchor new habitat nodes.

⬡

Waystation Holders

Monarch Watch and Homegrown National Park participants wanting to understand how their yards and gardens fit into the larger corridor picture.

⬡

Researchers & Developers

UW-Green Bay faculty, student researchers, and technical contributors extending the platform with new data sources or alert types.
