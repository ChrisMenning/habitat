The Problem

The data exists.
The view doesn't.

Pollinator conservation in northeastern Wisconsin is fragmented across agencies, organizations, and data systems.

The Green Bay Conservation Corps manages a regional corridor. The Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources oversees state natural areas and managed lands. Federal datasets track protected areas and bee distributions. Community scientists log tens of thousands of sightings annually — but no unified tool brings these sources together at the local scale.

Unifying that data at the local scale is a hard technical problem — one that conservation organizations shouldn't have to solve themselves. Bay Hive does.

◎
Neglected habitat sites

Sites that receive no documented pollinator activity may be unknowingly overlooked, with no systematic way to identify them.

◎
Unflagged hazard proximity

Contamination from per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFAS) near sensitive habitat is not systematically cross-referenced with corridor boundaries or waystations.

◎
Invisible restoration candidates

High-sighting parcels with no formal habitat designation — the best restoration candidates — remain invisible without a unified spatial view.

The Tool

One map.
All the data.

Bay Hive integrates seventeen authoritative public datasets into a continuously refreshed map centered on a 15-kilometer radius from Green Bay city center. Observation data refreshes hourly; area layers daily. No GIS software, no installation, no paid data subscriptions.

01

Multi-source sightings

iNaturalist (historical observations back to 2010 — up to 2,000 per calendar year), Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF) records (up to 600), eBird, and Wikimedia Commons photos — filterable by research grade, native species, and proximity to habitat.

02

Conservation layers

U.S. Geological Survey Protected Areas Database (USGS PAD-US) v3.0, Wisconsin Department of Natural Resources (WI DNR) State Natural Areas, DNR Managed Lands, Green Bay Conservation Corps (GBCC) Pollinator Corridor, and Habitat Treatment records in one view.

03

Bee distribution analysis

U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service (FWS) dataset across all 6 bee families, a species richness heatmap, and an imperiled species layer covering NatureServe G1–3 and International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) VU+.

04

Land cover & cropland

National Land Cover Database (NLCD) 2021 (16 toggleable classes), USDA Cropland Data Layer with bee-crop fringe heatmap, and WI DNR Urban Tree Canopy (2013, 2020, 2022).

05

Hazard overlay

WI DNR PFAS contamination sites with automated 1 km proximity alerts to habitat nodes and Monarch Watch Waystations.

06

Trend analysis & export

Year-over-year sighting charts once two or more observation snapshots are collected. Full plain-text intelligence report export at any time.

07

Expansion opportunities

0–100 scored candidate sites for new pollinator habitat where ecological conditions are favorable but no registered program exists within 800 m. Six-factor model: native plant records, nesting suitability, corridor proximity, PFAS-free environment, pesticide pressure, and pollinator activity. Three recommendation types guide who to contact.

08

Site signal diagnostics

Seven heuristic checks run against Corridor site data whenever the app loads — PFAS proximity, absence of nearby sightings, isolation, weak connections, nesting habitat scores, shade, and county-level pesticide pressure. These flag patterns worth looking into, not definitive diagnoses.

09

Landscape suitability & urban habitat indices

Two complementary layers derived from the InVEST Lonsdorf et al. (2009) pollinator model, which was originally designed and calibrated for agricultural landscapes. The Landscape Suitability Index (1.3 km grid) runs the model as intended — rural grassland and wetland naturally dominate, and urban corridor plantings are invisible at that resolution. The Urban Habitat Index is a derivative adaptation: the same kernel is re-run at a finer 660 m grid, restricted to developed land, and scored relative to other urban cells rather than rural habitat — making within-city habitat differences visible. Foraging Range Bands overlay concentric rings per corridor site for small, medium, and long-range bee guilds, creating an isobar-style view of where foraging zones converge.

A Note on This Tool

Early stage.
Genuinely useful.

Bay Hive is an open-source tool built by one person, in public, with the conservation community in mind.

The alert thresholds are a first pass — reasonable defaults built from published literature, not field-validated by corridor practitioners. The suitability models are directional indicators, not peer-reviewed outputs. The data it draws on is authoritative; the conclusions it draws from that data benefit from scrutiny.

What's here is genuinely useful for seeing patterns that are otherwise invisible at the local scale. It becomes more useful — and more trustworthy — with input from people who know this landscape. That's the ask.

◎
Alert logic needs field review

The spatial alerts represent a first pass. Practitioners with corridor knowledge can identify false positives, missing conditions, and thresholds that don't match reality on the ground.

◎
Models are directional, not definitive

The InVEST pollinator index and nesting suitability scores indicate relative conditions across the landscape. They are starting points for investigation, not conclusions.

◎
Built to be extended

The codebase is modular and dependency-free. New data sources, refined alert conditions, and additional layers can be added without toolchain setup. Contributions are welcome.

Who It's For

Built to start
conversations.

Bay Hive is an early-stage tool. The people described below are the audiences we hope to be useful to — and would genuinely like to hear from. If you work in any of these areas and see something worth improving, that feedback matters.

⬡

Habitat Coordinators

Staff managing corridor planting programs who might want a spatial view of how sites relate to each other, what the observation record looks like nearby, and where the data raises questions worth investigating in the field.

⬡

Agency Biologists

Resource managers who already work with several of the underlying datasets and might find value in seeing them overlaid alongside community observation records in a single regional view.

⬡

Community Scientists

iNaturalist and GBIF contributors who want to see their observations in the context of regional conservation work and corridor health.

⬡

Municipal Planners

Parks and planning staff interested in where public land overlaps with areas that have active pollinator sightings but no formal habitat designation — a starting point for conversation, not a site recommendation.

⬡

Waystation Holders

Monarch Watch and Homegrown National Park participants wanting to understand how their yards and gardens fit into the larger corridor picture.

⬡

Researchers

Anyone curious about how community-sourced observation data overlaps with formal habitat programs in the Green Bay region. The tool assembles the public record; field expertise is what makes it meaningful.

Governance

Open by design.
Local by intent.

"Bay Hive began as a personal project: one person, one afternoon, one question about where pollinators were showing up relative to the habitat people were building for them."

Bay Hive is released under an open license, ensuring it remains a public good even if adapted or stewarded by another organization. Bay Hive is deliberately local. The 15 km radius, the GBCC corridor data, the Brown County parcel integration — all of it is calibrated to this place. A local conservation organization stewarding a local tool is the right outcome. The goal is a tool that the Green Bay pollinator community owns, understands, and can maintain without depending on any single person.

Current Maintainer Chris Menning

Public data belongs to the public

Every layer is sourced from publicly available APIs and datasets. No proprietary data, no lock-in. The only thing Bay Hive adds is the assembly.

The tool serves the corridor, not the maintainer

Decisions about direction should be made in conversation with the people doing habitat work — coordinators, waystation holders, city planners, and researchers. That's a goal, not a current state.

Transparency by default

The code is open. The data sources are documented. The alert algorithms are explained in plain language in the app itself. There is no black box.

No growth for its own sake

The right question for any proposed feature: does this help someone doing habitat work make a better decision? Scope creep that serves ambition is not welcome.

Paths to stewardship

A single-maintainer structure is fine for an early-stage project. If Bay Hive ever grows into something the corridor community genuinely finds useful, the right long-term home for it is with that community. The question worth spending real time on is not whether Bay Hive should eventually be stewarded by a community organization — it should — but what kind of organization and what kind of relationship. Three pathways are currently under consideration, each suited to a different stage of the project’s maturity and a different level of organizational investment.

Pathway 01

Local Nonprofit Stewardship

Bay Hive’s codebase and development become the responsibility of an existing organization doing corridor work — most naturally the Green Bay Conservation Corps (GBCC). The cleanest transfer of stewardship: the people closest to the work maintain the tool that serves it.

Pathway 02

Purpose-Built Organization

A governing body with representation from GBCC, a conservation nonprofit, a local government partner, and a technical maintainer. Appropriate if the tool grows to serve a broader region.
