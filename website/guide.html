Reading the Map

Three kinds of site.
One connected corridor.

The map draws three overlapping habitat networks simultaneously: formally managed planting sites, registered private waystations, and native-planting yards enrolled in the national biodiversity program. Lines between nearby sites visualize whether pollinators of different size classes can actually traverse the gaps between them.

Habitat Site Types
GBCC · ArcGIS Feature Service

Pollinator Corridor Sites

Managed planting areas mapped by the Green Bay Conservation Corps as part of the NE Wisconsin Pollinator Corridor Project. Each site has a documented plant list, mapped area, and a named park or public property. These are the formal backbone of the regional habitat network and the primary targets of the app's alert engine — unsupported sites, weak nodes, and connectivity gaps are all measured relative to this layer.

Monarch Watch · Waystation Registry

Monarch Waystations

Registered habitat sites — home gardens, schoolyards, nature centers, community gardens, and places of worship — enrolled in the Monarch Watch Waystation Program. Each provides certified milkweed and nectar plants for monarchs and other pollinators. Locations were verified against Brown County parcel records by address and registrant name; approximate positions are flagged in the drawer and shown at address-range precision (± 50 m).

Homegrown National Park · Guest API

HNP Native Planting Yards

Yards and organizational properties enrolled in Doug Tallamy's Homegrown National Park initiative, indicating committed native plantings. While not formally mapped as managed habitat, HNP registrations reveal the latent network of private landowners already engaged in biodiversity restoration — an existing base of potential partners for formal corridor extension and restoration outreach.

The Site Dossier

Clicking any habitat site marker opens a persistent side drawer showing a full dossier for that location — assembled in real time from the data loaded into memory, with no additional network requests.

Dossiers are only available for active habitat program site markers — Corridor, Waystation, and HNP. Observation points (iNaturalist, GBIF) use a smaller popup instead.

  • Name & Source Site name, data source (GBCC, Monarch Watch, or HNP), and registration date if available.
  • Sighting Count Total pollinator sightings within 750 m across all loaded layers (iNat + GBIF), shown as a live count.
  • Seasonal Activity 12-bar histogram of sighting counts by calendar month — immediately shows which months have documented activity gaps.
  • Observed Species Species list grouped by data source (iNat, GBIF), sorted by observation count. Groups with more than 5 species have an expandable overflow.
  • Nesting Score NLCD-derived 0–100 index scoring bare ground, shrubland, and grassland within 300 m. Scores below 25 trigger the Poor Nesting Habitat alert.
  • Canopy Cover WI DNR 2022 urban tree canopy percentage within 150 m. Sites above 55% canopy may be too shaded for sun-loving pollinator plants.
  • Nearby Sites Other habitat sites within 750 m — types, distances, and names — giving an immediate read of local corridor density.
  • Nearby Parcels Brown County parcel records within 400 m, grouped by ownership (City, County, State, institutional, private) and linked to the assessor database.
Connectivity Lines & Foraging Ranges

A line is drawn between any two habitat sites within 2 km of each other. The line's visual weight and style encode whether pollinators of different size classes can realistically traverse that gap — informed by published foraging-range research for Midwestern native bee families. The thresholds are approximations, not hard ecological limits.

Optimal · ≤ 300 m · solid thick

Connected — accessible to all native bee species

Includes sweat bees (Halictidae), small mining bees (Andrenidae), mason bees (Osmia), and leafcutter bees (Megachile) — species whose foraging radius rarely exceeds 300–400 m. A solid thick line signals these two sites are within reach of even the shortest-ranging foragers. Dense clusters of optimal-range connections indicate a well-functioning corridor node.

Fair · 300–700 m · solid medium

Connected — accessible to bumble bees and large solitary bees

Accessible to bumble bees (Bombus spp.) and larger solitary species such as carpenter bees (Xylocopa) and large mason bees, whose comfortable foraging range extends to roughly 700 m. A medium-weight solid line signals a genuine functional connection, though small-bodied species may not reliably traverse the gap. These pairs are candidates for stepping-stone plantings that would bring them into the optimal tier.

Extended · 700 m–2 km · dashed thin

Connected — but with reduced species support

These sites are connected, but only larger-ranging bumble bees (Bombus pensylvanicus, B. griseocollis) typically traverse this distance during foraging bouts based on published range estimates, with a practical upper limit near 1.5–2 km. Small-bodied species — mining bees, sweat bees, and mason bees — may not cross this gap unaided. A dashed thin line reflects the reduced breadth of species supported. The app's Weak Node and Connectivity Gap alerts flag these pairs as priority candidates for stepping-stone restoration plantings.

Analysis Layers

The Analysis panel surfaces three computed layers that go beyond raw data to interpret network health and guide action.

⚡ Expansion Opportunities

Locations where ecological conditions support new habitat — but no registered program yet exists within 800 m. Native plant documentation and nesting habitat suitability are the primary signals; pollinator sightings serve only as supporting evidence (the goal of a new site is to attract pollinators, not to follow them).

Each marker is scored 0–100 across six factors:

  • Native plant records (primary, 0–35 pts) — documented native plants within 800 m of the candidate location (iNat, GBIF). A strong indicator: where native plants are documented, conditions may support pollinator habitat.
  • Nesting habitat suitability (primary, 0–30 pts) — proxied from the NLCD nesting score of the nearest scored corridor site within 3 km. High grassland and bare-ground cover predicts good ground-nesting conditions.
  • Corridor stepping-stone proximity (0–15 pts) — located 0.8–3 km from an existing Pollinator Corridor site, maximising connectivity value.
  • PFAS-free environment (0 or −20 pts) — no known PFAS detection within 1.5 km. Contaminated sites receive a heavy score penalty.
  • Low pesticide pressure (0 or −5 pts) — county not in the top-quartile agricultural pesticide band.
  • Pollinator activity (0–5 pts) — active sightings as a light supporting signal only.

Each marker also carries a Recommendation Type based on the character of the surrounding land:

  • Corridor Expansion Candidate (green) — Within stepping-stone range of an existing Corridor site on public land. Suitable for a formal Pollinator Corridor planting. Coordinate with City of Green Bay Parks.
  • Community Engagement Opportunity (purple) — Primarily near Waystation or HNP sites, suggesting private residential land. Consider outreach to property owners about Monarch Waystation registration or the Homegrown National Park program.
  • Dual-Pathway Opportunity (amber) — Mixed or unknown land character. Assess both formal Corridor eligibility and community engagement. A field visit is recommended.

⚠ Problem Areas

Corridor sites with one or more identified issues that may limit their value to the network. Problem areas are detected automatically from loaded data and updated whenever the app refreshes. Seven problem types are tracked:

  • PFAS Proximity (high) — Known PFAS detection within 1 km. Soil and water contamination risk for plantings and insects.
  • No Pollinator Activity (medium) — No recorded sightings within 500 m. May indicate low visibility, lack of observation effort, or genuine poor visitation.
  • Isolated Site (high) — No registered habitat of any type within 2 km. Likely beyond reliable foraging range for most species based on published estimates.
  • Weak Connection (medium) — Nearest neighbor 700 m–2 km away. Small-bodied bees (mining, sweat, mason) may not reliably traverse this gap based on typical foraging ranges.
  • Poor Nesting Habitat (medium) — NLCD-derived nesting suitability score below 25/100. Low bare ground and grassland cover within 300 m.
  • Shaded Habitat (low) — Over 55% tree canopy within 150 m may suppress sun-loving pollinator plants.
  • High Pesticide Pressure (high) — Site in a top-quartile agricultural pesticide county.

🌱 InVEST Pollinator Index

A continuous relative pollinator abundance index computed across the full 15 km study area using the Lonsdorf et al. (2009) foraging model (InVEST Pollinator Supply & Demand). Inputs are NLCD 2021 land cover biophysical scores — nesting suitability and floral resource availability estimated per class. Output is an amber-to-gold heatmap grid where higher values indicate landscapes more capable of supporting pollinator populations, independent of where observations have actually been recorded.

Three bee guilds are modeled simultaneously:

  • Short-range guild (0.3 km) — Sweat bees (Halictidae), small mining bees, and mason bees (Osmia spp.) whose typical foraging radius is 300 m or less.
  • Medium-range guild (0.7 km) — Large mason bees, leafcutter bees (Megachile), and bumble bee workers in moderate foraging conditions.
  • Long-range guild (1.5 km) — Bumble bees (Bombus pensylvanicus, B. griseocollis) and carpenter bees (Xylocopa) with extended foraging capacity.

Each guild computes nesting supply × weighted floral accessibility within its foraging radius. The three outputs are combined and normalized (0 = lowest, 1 = highest relative index). Off by default; toggle via the Layers panel.

Data Layers

The Layers panel surfaces every toggleable data source. Layers are organized into six groups, each covering a different aspect of the regional landscape and biodiversity picture.

Biodiversity Observations

  • iNaturalist — Research-grade sightings back to 2010, up to 2,000 records per calendar year. Four independently toggleable sub-layers: Pollinators (on by default), Native Plants, Other Plants, and Wildlife. Each dot’s outer ring indicates establishment status (native / introduced / invasive) resolved against Wisconsin’s regional checklist.
  • GBIF — Global Biodiversity Information Facility occurrence records (up to 600), including museum specimens and multi-source research datasets. Mirrors iNat’s four sub-layers. Useful for validating iNat coverage and finding historically documented species.
  • eBird — Cornell Lab of Ornithology bird sightings. Off by default. Requires an optional eBird API key configured in api-keys.txt.
  • Bee Records (FWS) — USGS/FWS dataset covering all six bee families. Three view modes: Bee Records (individual sample points, on by default), Species Richness (density heatmap), and Imperiled Species (NatureServe G1–G3 and IUCN VU+).
  • Commons Photos — Wikimedia Commons geotagged images within the 15 km radius. Clicking a camera marker opens a lightbox with the photo, attribution, and Commons link.

Conservation Areas

  • Protected Areas (PAD-US) — USGS Protected Areas Database v3.0. Federal, state, local, tribal, and NGO lands as polygon fills. Each popup shows managing entity, designation type, public access level, and GAP conservation status (1 = strictly protected → 4 = no formal protection).
  • State Natural Areas (SNA) — WI DNR designated sites preserving outstanding natural communities, often with rare plant species. On by default.
  • DNR Managed Lands — Broader DNR-managed properties: wildlife areas, state forests, parks. Off by default.
  • Pollinator Corridor (GBCC) — NE Wisconsin Pollinator Corridor Project planting site polygons from the Green Bay Conservation Corps. On by default. The backbone layer for all connectivity analysis.
  • Habitat Treatments (GBCC) — GBCC restoration interventions published to their public feature service. Current data includes cut-stump and foliar treatments. Shows the active restoration footprint. Off by default.

Land Cover & Agriculture

  • NLCD 2021 (16 classes) — National Land Cover Database at 30 m resolution via MRLC GeoServer WMS. Each class is individually toggleable — Open Water through Emergent Herbaceous Wetlands. Useful for identifying nesting substrate (bare ground, shrubland, grassland) near corridor sites. Also the input layer for the NLCD-derived nesting suitability score shown in the site dossier.
  • Cropland Data Layer (CDL) — USDA NASS CropScape raster (30 m resolution) showing dominant crop cover. Used with NASS QuickStats to compute the Pollinator–Crop Mismatch alert.
  • Tree Canopy (2013 / 2020 / 2022) — WI DNR Urban Tree Canopy classification in three annual snapshots. A scrubber control switches between vintages. Per-site canopy % (150 m radius) also drives the Shaded Habitat alert and appears in the site dossier. Note: WI DNR advises against direct change-detection comparisons across years.

Hazards

  • PFAS Contamination Sites — WI DNR-mapped PFAS detection and investigation sites in surface water and fish tissue. On by default. A 1 km proximity check to any corridor site or Monarch Waystation triggers the PFAS Near Habitat alert.
  • Pesticide Pressure — County-level choropleth using the USDA/NASS Critical Band index across 9 NE Wisconsin counties. Bands 1–4 (Low to Critical) reflect crop mix and associated neonicotinoid use. Any corridor site in a Critical-band county triggers the High Pesticide Pressure alert.

Parcel Ownership

Brown County parcel records classified into five ownership tiers: City of Green Bay, Brown County, State of Wisconsin, Institutional (schools, churches, non-profits), and Private. Off by default.

Clicking any public parcel opens a drawer with contact information for the responsible agency (City Parks, County Parks, or WI DNR) plus assessor links for the parcel record. The Expansion Opportunities layer uses parcel ownership to assign a Recommendation Type and to show public land badges in expansion drawers and export reports.
