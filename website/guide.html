Reading the Map

Three kinds of site.
One connected corridor.

The map draws three overlapping habitat networks simultaneously: formally managed planting sites, registered private waystations, and native-planting yards enrolled in the national biodiversity program. Lines between nearby sites visualize whether pollinators of different size classes can actually traverse the gaps between them.

Habitat Site Types
GBCC · ArcGIS Feature Service

Pollinator Corridor Sites

Managed planting areas mapped by the Green Bay Conservation Corps as part of the NE Wisconsin Pollinator Corridor Project. Each site has a documented plant list, mapped area, and a named park or public property. These are the formal backbone of the regional habitat network and the primary targets of the app's alert engine — unsupported sites, weak nodes, and connectivity gaps are all measured relative to this layer.

Monarch Watch · Waystation Registry

Monarch Waystations

Registered habitat sites — home gardens, schoolyards, nature centers, community gardens, and places of worship — enrolled in the Monarch Watch Waystation Program. Each provides certified milkweed and nectar plants for monarchs and other pollinators. Locations were verified against Brown County parcel records by address and registrant name; approximate positions are flagged in the drawer and shown at address-range precision (± 50 m).

Homegrown National Park · Guest API

HNP Native Planting Yards

Yards and organizational properties enrolled in Doug Tallamy's Homegrown National Park initiative, indicating committed native plantings. While not formally mapped as managed habitat, HNP registrations reveal the latent network of private landowners already engaged in biodiversity restoration — an existing base of potential partners for formal corridor extension and restoration outreach.

The Site Dossier

Clicking any habitat site marker opens a persistent side drawer showing a full dossier for that location — assembled in real time from the data loaded into memory, with no additional network requests.

Dossiers are only available for active habitat program site markers — Corridor, Waystation, and HNP. Observation points (iNaturalist, GBIF) use a smaller popup instead.

  • Name & Source Site name, data source (GBCC, Monarch Watch, or HNP), and registration date if available.
  • Sighting Count Total pollinator sightings within 750 m across all loaded layers (iNat + GBIF), shown as a live count.
  • Seasonal Activity 12-bar histogram of sighting counts by calendar month — immediately shows which months have documented activity gaps.
  • Observed Species Species list grouped by data source (iNat, GBIF), sorted by observation count. Groups with more than 5 species have an expandable overflow.
  • Nesting Score NLCD-derived 0–100 index scoring bare ground, shrubland, and grassland within 300 m. Scores below 25 trigger the Poor Nesting Habitat alert.
  • Canopy Cover WI DNR 2022 urban tree canopy percentage within 150 m. Sites above 55% canopy may be too shaded for sun-loving pollinator plants.
  • Nearby Sites Other habitat sites within 750 m — types, distances, and names — giving an immediate read of local corridor density.
  • Nearby Parcels Brown County parcel records within 400 m, grouped by ownership (City, County, State, institutional, private) and linked to the assessor database.
Connectivity Lines & Foraging Ranges

A line is drawn between any two habitat sites within 2 km of each other. The line's visual weight and style encode whether pollinators of different size classes can realistically traverse that gap — grounded in published foraging-range data for Midwestern native bee families.

Optimal · ≤ 300 m · solid thick

Connected — accessible to all native bee species

Includes sweat bees (Halictidae), small mining bees (Andrenidae), mason bees (Osmia), and leafcutter bees (Megachile) — species whose foraging radius rarely exceeds 300–400 m. A solid thick line signals these two sites are within reach of even the shortest-ranging foragers. Dense clusters of optimal-range connections indicate a well-functioning corridor node.

Fair · 300–700 m · solid medium

Connected — accessible to bumble bees and large solitary bees

Accessible to bumble bees (Bombus spp.) and larger solitary species such as carpenter bees (Xylocopa) and large mason bees, whose comfortable foraging range extends to roughly 700 m. A medium-weight solid line signals a genuine functional connection, though small-bodied species may not reliably traverse the gap. These pairs are candidates for stepping-stone plantings that would bring them into the optimal tier.

Extended · 700 m–2 km · dashed thin

Connected — but with reduced species support

These sites are connected, but only larger-ranging bumble bees (Bombus pensylvanicus, B. griseocollis) reliably traverse this distance during foraging bouts, with a practical upper limit near 1.5–2 km. Small-bodied species — mining bees, sweat bees, and mason bees — may not cross this gap unaided. A dashed thin line reflects the reduced breadth of species supported. The app's Weak Node and Connectivity Gap alerts flag these pairs as priority candidates for stepping-stone restoration plantings.

Analysis Layers

The Analysis panel surfaces three computed layers that go beyond raw data to interpret network health and guide action.

⚡ Expansion Opportunities

Locations where ecological conditions support new habitat — but no registered program yet exists within 800 m. Native plant documentation and nesting habitat suitability are the primary signals; pollinator sightings serve only as supporting evidence (the goal of a new site is to attract pollinators, not to follow them).

Each marker is scored 0–100 across six factors:

  • Native plant records (primary, 0–35 pts) — documented native plants within 800 m of the candidate location (iNat, GBIF). The strongest ecological signal: where native plants thrive, pollinator habitat can be established.
  • Nesting habitat suitability (primary, 0–30 pts) — proxied from the NLCD nesting score of the nearest scored corridor site within 3 km. High grassland and bare-ground cover predicts good ground-nesting conditions.
  • Corridor stepping-stone proximity (0–15 pts) — located 0.8–3 km from an existing Pollinator Corridor site, maximising connectivity value.
  • PFAS-free environment (0 or −20 pts) — no known PFAS detection within 1.5 km. Contaminated sites receive a heavy score penalty.
  • Low pesticide pressure (0 or −5 pts) — county not in the top-quartile agricultural pesticide band.
  • Pollinator activity (0–5 pts) — active sightings as a light supporting signal only.

Each marker also carries a Recommendation Type based on the character of the surrounding land:

  • Corridor Expansion Candidate (green) — Within stepping-stone range of an existing Corridor site on public land. Suitable for a formal Pollinator Corridor planting. Coordinate with City of Green Bay Parks.
  • Community Engagement Opportunity (purple) — Primarily near Waystation or HNP sites, suggesting private residential land. Consider outreach to property owners about Monarch Waystation registration or the Homegrown National Park program.
  • Dual-Pathway Opportunity (amber) — Mixed or unknown land character. Assess both formal Corridor eligibility and community engagement. A field visit is recommended.

⚠ Problem Areas

Corridor sites with one or more identified issues that may limit their value to the network. Problem areas are detected automatically from loaded data and updated whenever the app refreshes. Seven problem types are tracked:

  • PFAS Proximity (high) — Known PFAS detection within 1 km. Soil and water contamination risk for plantings and insects.
  • No Pollinator Activity (medium) — No recorded sightings within 500 m. May indicate low visibility, lack of observation effort, or genuine poor visitation.
  • Isolated Site (high) — No registered habitat of any type within 2 km. Beyond reliable bumble bee foraging range; unable to exchange pollinators with the network.
  • Weak Connection (medium) — Nearest neighbor 700 m–2 km away. Small-bodied bees (mining, sweat, mason) cannot reliably traverse this gap.
  • Poor Nesting Habitat (medium) — NLCD-derived nesting suitability score below 25/100. Low bare ground and grassland cover within 300 m.
  • Shaded Habitat (low) — Over 55% tree canopy within 150 m may suppress sun-loving pollinator plants.
  • High Pesticide Pressure (high) — Site in a top-quartile agricultural pesticide county.

🌱 Habitat Suitability Heatmap

A green-ramp heatmap showing where habitat establishment is most likely to succeed across the full 15 km study area. Native plant documentation is the primary positive signal; pollinator activity is a light supporting factor only. Bright green = high suitability; faint or absent = insufficient ecological basis or contamination penalty.

Scoring per grid cell (∼1.7 km spacing):

  • Native plants (0–0.45) — Dominant factor. Requires at least one native plant record to register.
  • Stepping-stone proximity (0–0.22) — 0.5–3 km from existing habitat.
  • Pollinator activity (0–0.12) — Supporting signal only.
  • PFAS penalty (−0.30) — Contaminated areas are suppressed on the heatmap.
  • Pesticide penalty (−0.15) — High-pressure counties are scored down.
