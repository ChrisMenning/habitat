Alert Engine

Fourteen
automated
spatial alerts

The platform's core analytical output: fourteen spatial checks that run entirely in the browser against data already loaded in memory, with no server-side processing required.

All alert logic is client-side only, documented in plain language within the app itself, and designed for field review by practitioners who can identify false positives or missing conditions.
PFAS
PFAS Near Habitat

Contamination site within 1 km of any corridor node or Monarch Watch Waystation (Haversine distance).

Habitat
Unsupported Sites

Corridor sites with no documented pollinator sighting within 500 m.

Habitat
Opportunity Zones

~1 km grid cells with ≥5 sightings and no formal habitat designation within 800 m — restoration candidates.

Habitat
Public Land Gap

City/County parcel ≥0.5 ac inside an opportunity zone with no nearby habitat — highest-priority acquisition targets.

Habitat
Declining Public Zone

Opportunity zone with a declining sighting trend and publicly owned land nearby.

Connect
Connected Pairs

Habitat site pairs within 300 m — corridor stepping-stone distance.

Connect
Isolated Habitat

Habitat site with no other habitat within 2 km — isolated from the corridor network.

Connect
Weak Nodes

Corridor sites whose nearest neighbor is 700 m–2 km away, indicating a weakly connected network position.

Connect
Connectivity Gap

Widest edge in the corridor's minimum spanning tree — flagged when it exceeds 2.5 km.

Connect
Quadrant Coverage

Four quadrants around map centroid; flagged when any quadrant has no designated habitat.

Crops
Pollinator–Crop Mismatch

Bee-dependent crop acreage in areas without adequate estimated habitat coverage nearby.

Site
High Pesticide Pressure

Corridor sites in top-quartile pesticide-pressure counties per the Critical Band index.

Site
Poor Nesting Habitat

Sites scoring below 25/100 on the NLCD-derived nesting suitability index.

Site
Shaded Habitat

Corridor sites with >55% tree canopy coverage within 150 m (WI DNR Urban Tree Canopy 2022).

Governance

Open by design.
Local by intent.

"Bay Hive began as a personal project: one person, one afternoon, one question about where pollinators were showing up relative to the habitat people were building for them."

Tools that serve a community should be governed by that community, not held in trust by whoever happened to build them first. Bay Hive is released under the GNU General Public License v3.0 — ensuring it remains a public good even if forked or adapted by another organization. An organization that takes stewardship cannot close the source, cannot relicense it as proprietary.

Bay Hive is deliberately local. The 15 km radius, the GBCC corridor data, the Brown County parcel integration — all of it is calibrated to this place. A local conservation organization stewarding a local tool is the right outcome. The goal is a tool that the Green Bay pollinator community owns, understands, and can maintain without depending on any single person.

Current Maintainer Chris Menning

Public data belongs to the public

Every layer is sourced from publicly available APIs and datasets. No proprietary data, no lock-in. The only thing Bay Hive adds is the assembly.

The tool serves the corridor, not the maintainer

Decisions about direction should be made in the interest of the people doing habitat work — coordinators, waystation holders, city planners, researchers at UW-Green Bay.

Transparency by default

The code is open. The data sources are documented. The alert algorithms are explained in plain language in the app itself. There is no black box.

No growth for its own sake

The right question for any proposed feature: does this help someone doing habitat work make a better decision? Scope creep that serves ambition is not welcome.

Pathway 01

Fiscal Sponsorship

A 501(c) fiscal host administers funds while the project retains operational independence. Enables grant applications and tax-deductible donations. Candidates include Open Source Collective and Aspiration Tech.

Pathway 02

Local Nonprofit Stewardship

Bay Hive's codebase and development become the responsibility of an existing organization doing corridor work — most naturally the Green Bay Conservation Corps. The cleanest transfer of stewardship.

Pathway 03

Purpose-Built Organization

A governing body with representation from GBCC, a conservation nonprofit, a local government partner, and a technical maintainer seat. Appropriate if the tool grows beyond Green Bay.

Get Involved

The data is already
public.

The most valuable near-term contributions are field review of the fourteen alert algorithms — identifying false positives or missing conditions that practitioners recognize in the corridor — and integration of additional WI DNR or locally maintained datasets. The modular codebase requires no toolchain setup to extend.

⚐
Alert Field Review

Practitioners with field knowledge of the corridor can identify false positive patterns or alert conditions that the current algorithms miss. This is the highest-value contribution Bay Hive can receive.

⊕
Data Integration

Additional WI DNR datasets, parcel ownership records, or locally maintained habitat inventories can be incorporated as new layers or alert inputs by any contributor.

◷
Trend Harvesting

Organizations that deploy Bay Hive as a hosted service can schedule regular observation snapshots via POST /api/harvest to build the longitudinal dataset needed for multi-year trend analysis.

⌥
Technical Extension

The modular ES-module architecture makes it straightforward to add new data sources, refine the nesting suitability scoring model, or build new analytical layers — no build step or toolchain setup required.
