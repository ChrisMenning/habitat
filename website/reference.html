Alert Engine

Seventeen
automated
spatial alerts

The platform's core analytical output: seventeen spatial checks that run entirely in the browser against data already loaded in memory, with no server-side processing required.

All alert logic is client-side only, documented in plain language within the app itself, and designed for field review by practitioners who can identify false positives or missing conditions.

PFAS Near Habitat

~1 km grid cells with ≥5 sightings and no formal habitat designation within 800 m — restoration candidates.

City/County parcel ≥0.5 ac inside an opportunity zone with no nearby habitat — highest-priority acquisition targets.

Habitat site pairs within 300 m — corridor stepping-stone distance.

Isolated Habitat

Habitat site with no other habitat within 2 km — isolated from the corridor network.

Weak Nodes

Corridor sites whose nearest neighbor is 700 m–2 km away, indicating a weakly connected network position.

Connect
Connectivity Gap

Widest edge in the corridor's minimum spanning tree — flagged when it exceeds 2.5 km.

Connect
Quadrant Coverage

Four quadrants around map centroid; flagged when any quadrant has no designated habitat.

Crops
Pollinator–Crop Mismatch

Bee-dependent crop acreage in areas without adequate estimated habitat coverage nearby.

Site
High Pesticide Pressure

Corridor sites in top-quartile pesticide-pressure counties per the Critical Band index.

Site
Poor Nesting Habitat

Sites scoring below 25/100 on the National Land Cover Database (NLCD)-derived nesting suitability index.

Site
Shaded Habitat

Corridor sites with >55% tree canopy coverage within 150 m (WI DNR Urban Tree Canopy 2022).

Climate
Growing Degree Days (GDD) Below Normal

Daily growing-degree accumulation (base 50°F) running below the 30-year normal for the current May–September window at Green Bay. GDD is computed from IEM observed temperature data and does not require the NOAA optional key.

Climate
Fall Frost Risk

At least one sub-34°F forecast within the next 14-day NOAA window during the active pollinator season (May–Sep). Late frosts threaten late-season milkweed and nectar resources. Requires NOAA optional key.

Data
Temporal Mismatch

A loaded data layer whose vintage year is more than 3 years old while live-rendered on the map. Flagged to prompt users to verify whether older data is still representative of current conditions.

Governance

Open by design.
Local by intent.

"Bay Hive began as a personal project: one person, one afternoon, one question about where pollinators were showing up relative to the habitat people were building for them."

Bay Hive is released under an open license, ensuring it remains a public good even if adapted or stewarded by another organization. Bay Hive is deliberately local. The 15 km radius, the GBCC corridor data, the Brown County parcel integration — all of it is calibrated to this place. A local conservation organization stewarding a local tool is the right outcome. The goal is a tool that the Green Bay pollinator community owns, understands, and can maintain without depending on any single person.

Current Maintainer Chris Menning

Public data belongs to the public

Every layer is sourced from publicly available APIs and datasets. No proprietary data, no lock-in. The only thing Bay Hive adds is the assembly.

The tool serves the corridor, not the maintainer

Decisions about direction should be made in the interest of the people doing habitat work — coordinators, waystation holders, city planners, and researchers.

Transparency by default

The code is open. The data sources are documented. The alert algorithms are explained in plain language in the app itself. There is no black box.

No growth for its own sake

The right question for any proposed feature: does this help someone doing habitat work make a better decision? Scope creep that serves ambition is not welcome.

Paths to stewardship

A single-maintainer structure is fine for an early-stage project. It is not sustainable for a tool that a conservation committee, a city parks department, or a grant-funded corridor program might come to depend on. The question worth spending real time on is not whether Bay Hive should eventually be stewarded by a community organization — it should — but what kind of organization and what kind of relationship. Three pathways are currently under consideration, each suited to a different stage of the project’s maturity and a different level of organizational investment.

Pathway 01

Local Nonprofit Stewardship

Bay Hive’s codebase and development become the responsibility of an existing organization doing corridor work — most naturally the Green Bay Conservation Corps (GBCC). The cleanest transfer of stewardship: the people closest to the work maintain the tool that serves it.

Pathway 02

Purpose-Built Organization

A governing body with representation from GBCC, a conservation nonprofit, a local government partner, and a technical maintainer. Appropriate if the tool grows to serve a broader region.

Get Involved

The most valuable near-term contributions are field review of the automated alert algorithms — identifying false positives or missing conditions that practitioners recognize in the corridor — and integration of additional Wisconsin DNR or locally maintained datasets.

⚐
Alert Field Review

Practitioners with field knowledge of the corridor can identify false positive patterns or alert conditions that the current algorithms miss. This is the highest-value contribution Bay Hive can receive.

⊕
Data Integration

Additional Wisconsin DNR datasets, parcel ownership records, or locally maintained habitat inventories can be incorporated as new layers or alert inputs by any contributor.
