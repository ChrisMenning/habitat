Bay Hive — Feature Guide

Quick Start

Bay Hive opens with corridor sites, waystations, protected areas, and the pesticide/crop heatmaps already visible. To see live pollinator observations, click Load Observations in the left panel — this fetches iNaturalist and GBIF sightings for the 15 km Green Bay analysis area. Alerts on the right update automatically after each load.

Three high-value first steps: (1) Click Load Observations. (2) Open the Alerts panel to see what the data is surfacing. (3) Click any amber corridor site polygon on the map to open its full dossier — including nesting score, tree canopy coverage, nearby sightings, and network connectivity context.

eBird sightings, NOAA climate data, and NASS crop statistics require free API keys in api-keys.txt. The app works completely without them — those specific layers simply won't populate.

What Loads Automatically vs. On Demand

These load in the background when the page opens:

  • NE WI Pollinator Corridor, Waystations, HNP yards — area layers, 24 h browser cache
  • Conservation areas — USGS PAD-US, WI DNR State Natural Areas, DNR Managed Lands
  • Pesticide Pressure choropleth — on by default
  • Bee-Crop Field Fringe heatmap — on by default
  • Nesting suitability scores — fetched async immediately after corridor GeoJSON loads; appear as badge circles on corridor markers once any NLCD layer is toggled on
  • Tree canopy coverage % — fetched async after corridor data; used in the site drawer and Shaded Habitat alert even if the visual raster overlay is off

These require a click or toggle:

  • iNaturalist, GBIF, eBird observations — click Load Observations
  • Tree Canopy raster overlay — toggle in Land Cover & Cropland panel
  • Wikimedia Commons photos — enable Commons toggle in Conservation & Hazards
  • Parcel ownership data — enable Parcels toggle, then zoom to level 13 or higher

Habitat Programs Layer

  • NE WI Pollinator Corridor (GBCC) — amber filled polygons with circular site markers. Formal restoration plantings managed by the Green Bay Conservation Corps on public parks, rights-of-way, and institutional properties. Click any marker to open the full site dossier.
  • Monarch Watch Waystations — violet circles. 56 privately certified habitat sites; Monarch Watch certification requires a minimum milkweed and nectar planting.
  • Homegrown National Park yards — teal triangles. Self-reported native plant yards from Doug Tallamy's HNP initiative. No inspection required — homeowner commitments to plant native species that support local food webs.
  • GBCC Habitat Treatments — teal polygon outlines. Sites where GBCC has performed invasive removal, prescribed burns, or replanting — the active restoration footprint separate from established corridor plantings.

Site marker outer ring: Encodes establishment status from source data — green = native/established planting, orange = introduced or early-stage, gray = status not recorded. This is a data attribute, not a quality rating assigned by Bay Hive.

Nesting suitability badge: A small colored circle on each corridor marker shows a 0–100 score (see Site Detail Drawer for formula). Appears when any NLCD layer is active.

Conservation Areas & Hazards

  • USGS PAD-US Protected Areas — all federally, state, locally, and NGO-managed protected lands. Click a polygon for managing entity, designation type, public access level, and GAP conservation status (1 = strictly protected → 4 = no formal protection).
  • WI DNR State Natural Areas — sites preserved for outstanding natural communities; many contain rare plants and high-quality native habitat.
  • WI DNR Managed Lands — wildlife areas, state forests, and parks managed for broader conservation and recreation.
  • WI DNR PFAS Contamination Sites — red warning markers at PFAS detection sites in surface water and fish tissue. The PFAS Near Habitat alert fires for any site within 1 km of a corridor or waystation. Click a marker for the site name and detection context.
  • Pesticide Pressure choropleth — county-level intensity based on crop mix and neonicotinoid seed treatment patterns. Four bands: Low, Moderate, High, Critical. On by default. The High Pesticide Pressure alert fires when corridor sites or waystations fall in a Critical-band county.
  • Parcel ownership — Brown County parcel data (visible at zoom ≥ 13). Enables the Public Land Gap alert, which identifies publicly owned unimproved parcels near opportunity zones as actionable outreach targets.

Sightings Panel

  • Pollinators — bees, butterflies, moths, hoverflies; includes hummingbirds (ruby-throated hummingbirds pollinate native trumpet vine and cardinal flower)
  • Native Plants — flowering plants and host plants with native status confirmed against Wisconsin's regional iNaturalist checklist
  • Other Plants — introduced and invasive plant species; useful for spotting competition zones at corridor restoration sites
  • Other Wildlife — non-pollinator fauna
  • Bee Records — FWS Bee Distribution Tool dataset; museum specimens and research records across all 6 bee families
  • Bee Imperiled — species rated G1–G3 by NatureServe or VU+ by IUCN

The outer ring on iNaturalist dots: ● green = native confirmed, ● orange = introduced/invasive, ● gray = unresolved.

Date range: Use From/To inputs to filter by observation date. The debounce auto-re-fetches after a brief pause. The timeline scrubber at the bottom filters without re-fetching and also controls the active Tree Canopy survey year.

Filter chips: Narrow loaded observations without re-fetching — Research grade only, Near a habitat site (within 300 m), Native species only, or Non-native/invasive only.

Bee Records and Bee Imperiled are in the Sightings panel. Species Richness heatmap is in the Analysis panel.

Land Cover & Cropland

  • NLCD 2021 — 16 individually toggleable land cover classes at 30-meter resolution. Isolate a single class (e.g., Grassland/Herbaceous class 71) to see how much is present near corridor sites — useful context for interpreting low nesting scores.
  • CDL 2023 — USDA full-color crop type raster at 30-meter resolution. Source data for the Pollinator Mismatch alert.
  • Tree Canopy — WI DNR Urban Tree Canopy raster at 1-meter resolution, 3 survey years: 2013, 2020, 2022. Active year tracks the timeline end-year (nearest ≤ selected year). Toggle on/off here. Canopy % per corridor site is computed independently and appears in the site drawer and Shaded Habitat alert even when this visual overlay is off.

Analysis Overlays

  • Bee-Crop Field Fringe — amber heatmap of NLCD agricultural pixels (Pasture/Hay + Cultivated Crops) near the corridor. Warmer = higher crop pollination demand. On by default.
  • Corridor Proximity Bands — soft green glow between corridor nodes within 300 m of each other. 300 m is the practical stepping-stone threshold — pairs within this distance form a continuous habitat network rather than isolated islands.
  • Pollinator Access Traffic — amber/orange density heatmap of all active habitat sites. Gaps in the heatmap indicate underserved areas.
  • Nesting Habitat Badges — 0–100 score circles on corridor markers. See Site Detail Drawer for the full formula. Appear when any NLCD layer is active.
  • Species Richness — bee species richness density surface from the FWS Bee Distribution dataset. Shows where taxonomic diversity is highest, independent of raw counts.

Intel Bar — Situational Summary

Six live stats updated after each observation load. Five are clickable and open a focused info drawer:

  • Corridor area — total GBCC corridor polygon area in acres. Click for a per-site breakdown.
  • Habitat nodes — total active sites (corridor + waystations + HNP yards). Click for a count by type. The primary network density metric.
  • Pollinators sighted — combined iNat + GBIF pollinator count for the current date range. Click for source breakdown and top species.
  • Season / GDD — current Growing Degree Days vs. 1991–2020 normal with deviation %. Click to open the full Climate Ribbon chart.
  • eBird sightings — eBird observation count (requires API key). Click for species list.
  • Native species — unique native plant species observed across iNat + GBIF native plant layers, deduplicated by scientific name. Click for the list.
  • Alerts — active alert count. Pulses when present. A badge on the Alerts activity bar button also shows this count.

Interpretation tip: If Native species is low while Pollinators sighted is high, pollinators may be relying heavily on non-native plants. Enable the Other Plants layer and check for invasive species density near corridor sites — introduced plants can draw pollinators away from the native-species habitat corridor plantings are designed to support.

Alerts Panel — All 14 Alerts Explained

All alerts are computed client-side from data already loaded in memory — no extra fetches. Click any alert to fly the map to the affected area.

  • PFAS Near Habitat — a PFAS contamination site is within 1 km of a corridor site or waystation. Names both the contaminant site and the affected habitat. Action: inform site managers; inspect nearby surface water sources.
  • Unsupported Sites — corridor sites with no pollinator sightings within 500 m. May indicate under-surveying. Action: schedule an iNaturalist bioblitz at those sites to establish baseline records.
  • Opportunity Zones — ≥5 pollinator sightings in a ~1 km cell with no habitat program site within 800 m. Where pollinators are active but infrastructure isn't. Action: propose a new corridor site, waystation, or HNP yard there.
  • Declining Public Zone — an opportunity zone with >30% year-over-year sighting decline AND a City or County parcel within 800 m. Highest-priority target: documented decline + public ownership = a strong data-backed pitch to Parks.
  • Public Land Gap — a City or County parcel ≥0.5 acres inside an opportunity zone with no habitat within 500 m. Zoom to 13+ and enable Parcels to get the address and owner for a specific outreach pitch.
  • Connected Pairs — corridor site pairs within 300 m. Confirmed stepping-stone connections; more pairs = more resilient corridor.
  • Isolated Habitat — a corridor site with no other corridor site within 2 km. Most native bees can't reliably bridge >2 km. Action: identify a new planting ≤2 km away to reconnect it.
  • Weak Nodes — nearest corridor neighbor is 700 m–2 km away. Within foraging range but near the limit. Action: a midpoint planting upgrades these from stressed to solid.
  • Connectivity Gap — longest edge in the Minimum Spanning Tree of all corridor sites exceeds 2.5 km. The true network bottleneck. Action: bridging that MST gap is the single highest-leverage new planting location.
  • Regional Gap — one or more geographic quadrants has no habitat sites. Action: even one HNP registration in that quadrant begins coverage.
  • Pollinator Mismatch — bee-dependent crop acreage is high relative to estimated habitat coverage. The economic leverage case for new sites — corridor additions near these fields improve crop yields.
  • High Pesticide Pressure — corridor sites or waystations in a Critical-band county. Action: prioritize pesticide-tolerant nurse species in plantings; avoid recommending nearby water sources.
  • Poor Nesting Habitat — corridor sites below 25/100 on the NLCD nesting index (little bare ground, shrubland, or grassland within 300 m). Action: add bare-soil patches, sand berms, or let mowed edges revert to grassland.
  • Shaded Habitat — corridor sites with >55% tree canopy within 150 m (WI DNR UTC 2022). Heavy shading suppresses sun-loving corridor plants (prairie clover, coneflower, goldenrod all need full sun). Action: discuss selective canopy thinning with the site manager.

Site Detail Drawer

Click any feature on the map to open the drawer. For corridor sites it shows:

  • Coordinates and area — WGS84 decimal degrees and polygon area in acres.
  • Nesting suitability score — 0–100 score from NLCD 2021 within 300 m. Click next to the section label for the full formula. Gray ≤ 33 = Low (alert fires below 25), Tan 34–66 = Moderate, Dark brown ≥ 67 = Good.
  • Tree canopy coverage — % of the ~150 m radius covered by tree canopy (WI DNR UTC 2022). Click for the pixel-counting methodology. Values above 55% trigger the Shaded Habitat alert.
  • Corridor connectivity — nearest corridor neighbor distances: strong (≤300 m), acceptable (≤700 m), weak (≤2 km), or isolated (>2 km).
  • Nearby parcel ownership — Brown County parcel data near the site (when Parcels layer is enabled at zoom ≥ 13). Shows owner, acreage, and address.
  • Sighting activity — pollinator observation counts within 750 m with a monthly histogram.
  • Nearby sites — other habitat program sites within 2 km with distances.
  • Wikimedia Commons photos — nearby community photos when the Commons layer is enabled.

Trends Pane

Year-over-year charts appear once ≥2 harvested snapshot years exist. To populate: POST to /api/harvest with { "source": "inat", "year": 2024 }. Valid sources: inat, gbif, noaa, nass, cdl. Snapshots write to ./snapshots/ on the server.

Caching & Export

Area layers cached 24 h in IndexedDB; observations cached 1 h per date range. Intel bar shows Cached vs. Live. To force a re-fetch, change the date range or clear IndexedDB in browser DevTools → Application → Storage.

The Export button generates a plain-text intelligence report of the current map state — useful for briefing collaborators or documenting a site assessment.

Use Case: Finding the Right Location for a New Planting

You're advising the GBCC on where the corridor would benefit most from a new active restoration site.

  1. Load Observations with a broad date range (e.g., 2018–2025) to maximize sighting density data.
  2. Open Alerts. Look for Opportunity Zone alerts — where pollinators are active but habitat infrastructure is absent.
  3. Check for Public Land Gap alerts. These flag specific City or County parcels inside opportunity zones — public ownership means outreach to Parks & Recreation rather than private negotiation.
  4. Look for a Connectivity Gap alert. A new planting midway between two distant corridor sites that also overlaps an opportunity zone maximizes return on every restoration dollar.
  5. Click an alert to fly the map there. Enable Parcels layer and zoom to 13+. Click a public parcel polygon to get the owner name, acreage, and site address for a specific outreach pitch.

Example: The northeast quadrant shows a Regional Gap (no habitat sites), an Opportunity Zone with 9 bumblebee sightings, and a 0.8-acre City of Green Bay parks parcel on a road right-of-way 400 m from the opportunity cluster. Specific pitch: this parcel has documented native bee activity, no current habitat program, and sits in a service gap. A corridor planting here closes the regional gap, bridges a connectivity weakness, and brings infrastructure to where pollinators already are.

Use Case: Diagnosing an Underperforming Corridor Site

A corridor site is showing an Unsupported Sites alert — no pollinator observations within 500 m despite several years of available data.

  1. Click the alert to fly to the site, or click the amber corridor polygon directly.
  2. In the site drawer, check the Nesting suitability score. Low (below 34) means little bare ground or grassland — ground-nesting bees have nowhere to establish nests near the site.
  3. Check Tree canopy coverage. Above 55% means the site is heavily shaded — prairie clover, coneflower, black-eyed Susan, and goldenrod all require full sun (6+ hours). A shaded site will have poor establishment regardless of planting quality.
  4. Look at the Connectivity section. If the nearest corridor neighbor is >2 km, colonization pressure from nearby nesting populations is low — the site may simply need more time and nearby habitat to build bee populations.
  5. Enable NLCD Developed High and Developed Medium to check whether dense impervious surface surrounds the site, affecting both foraging patterns and microclimate.

Example: A NWTC corridor site scores 18/100 nesting (Low), 67% canopy (High — Shaded Habitat alert active), and nearest corridor neighbor is 1.9 km (Weak Node). Three compounding problems. Recommendation: work with NWTC facilities to selectively thin canopy around the planting boundary; add a bare-soil patch with fine gravel at the sunniest corner; propose a second corridor registration at the NWTC athletics complex 1.2 km west to shorten the gap to a solid 700 m.

Use Case: Reading the Season Before a Site Visit

You're coordinating a volunteer maintenance day and want to know whether planting season is running ahead, behind, or on schedule.

  1. Check the Season / GDD stat in the intel bar. A negative deviation (e.g., "–22%") means heat unit accumulation is behind the 30-year average — bloom timing and bee emergence are running late.
  2. Click the Season stat to open the Climate Ribbon. The solid gold line is current-year GDD; the dashed line is the 1991–2020 normal. Shaded amber regions are fall frost probability windows.
  3. Check for a Fall Frost Risk alert. If today is within 14 days of the P50 first frost date, annual plantings are approaching end-of-season and late interventions may not justify the effort.
  4. If GDD is well below normal (May–September), a GDD Below Normal alert shows with the deficit %. Use this to set volunteer expectations: later blooms, lower early-bee abundance, and delayed warm-season grass establishment.

Example: May 16, intel bar shows season is 22% below normal GDD — equivalent to early May in a normal year. Decision: delay transplanting warm-season annuals (anise hyssop, black-eyed Susan starts) by 2 weeks. Schedule iNaturalist bioblitz counts for early June rather than mid-May to align with peak early-bee emergence under this year's delayed conditions.

About Bay Hive

How This Started

Bay Hive began as a small personal experiment: I wanted to see iNaturalist pollinator sightings plotted on the same map as Green Bay's Monarch Waystation locations. That mashup took an afternoon, and it looked interesting — you could immediately see where sightings were clustering near certified habitat and where there were surprising gaps.

Then I found the Green Bay Conservation Corps pollinator corridor data. Then the USGS Protected Areas Database. Then Wisconsin DNR's State Natural Areas. Each new public dataset revealed another angle on the same question: where is the pollinator habitat, and where does it need to grow? The map kept getting more useful, so I kept building.

I volunteer helping weed and maintain the pollinator habitat planting at NWTC (Northeast Wisconsin Technical College), and I maintain my own pollinator habitat in my front yard. This project grew directly out of wanting to understand the bigger picture of what we're all collectively building across the Green Bay area.

This tool didn't exist before. I thought it should. So I made it.

Data Sources

Every layer in Bay Hive is sourced from publicly available APIs and datasets. Here's what each one is and how it's used:

🌸 iNaturalist — Pollinator & Plant Sightings

Source: iNaturalist API v1

The primary biodiversity data source. Up to 2,000 observations per query are fetched within 15 km of Green Bay. Observations are filtered and partitioned into four layers: Pollinators (bees, butterflies, moths, hoverflies, hummingbirds), Native Plants, Other Plants, and Other Wildlife. The establishment_means field is resolved against Wisconsin's regional checklist to determine native/introduced/invasive status, which drives the colored outer ring on each sighting dot. Data is cached by date range for 1 hour.

🔬 GBIF — Historical Biodiversity Records

Source: GBIF Occurrence API v1

Museum specimens, herbarium records, and multi-source research datasets provide historical depth that iNaturalist alone can't offer. GBIF pollinators and native/non-native plants are fetched separately (up to 600 records each). These appear as slightly different-styled dots below the iNaturalist layer so current active observations stay visually dominant.

🦋 Monarch Watch Certified Waystations

Source: Manually curated list cross-referenced with Brown County parcel records

56 Monarch Watch certified waystation habitats in the Green Bay area. Locations have been verified against public parcel data. These are private gardens and yards that have been formally certified as monarch butterfly habitat — each one a small node in the larger network. Displayed as violet circles with butterfly icons.

🌿 Homegrown National Park (HNP)

Source: Homegrown National Park guest API

Registered native plant yards from Doug Tallamy's Homegrown National Park initiative. These are self-reported yards where homeowners have committed to planting native species that support local food webs. Fetched via a CORS proxy in the local server since the HNP API doesn't send CORS headers. Proxied at /api/hnp-plantings.

🦋 NE Wisconsin Pollinator Corridor (GBCC)

Source: Green Bay Conservation Corps ArcGIS Feature Service

The mapped habitat planting areas that form the NE Wisconsin Pollinator Corridor. These are the formal program sites managed by the Green Bay Conservation Corps — public parks, rights-of-way, and institutional properties that have been planted with pollinator-supporting native species. This is the backbone of the corridor network the rest of the map is built around.

🌱 GBCC Habitat Treatments

Source: Green Bay Conservation Corps ArcGIS Feature Service

Restoration treatment polygons — sites where the GBCC has performed invasive removal, prescribed burns, or re-planting work. Shows the active restoration footprint separate from the established corridor planting areas.

🛡️ USGS PAD-US Protected Areas

Source: USGS PAD-US v3.0 ArcGIS Feature Service

The Protected Areas Database of the United States — the most comprehensive national inventory of public lands and waters protected for conservation. Federal, state, local government, tribal, NGO, and joint-managed lands are all included. Displayed as green polygon fills. Each popup shows the managing entity, designation type, public access level, and GAP conservation status (1 = strictly protected → 4 = no formal protection).

🌳 WI DNR State Natural Areas

Source: Wisconsin DNR ArcGIS MapServer

Wisconsin's network of State Natural Areas — sites preserved specifically to protect outstanding examples of Wisconsin's natural communities. Many SNAs contain rare plant species and high-quality natural habitat. Displayed as blue-teal polygon fills with acreage in popups.

🏕️ WI DNR Managed Lands

Source: Wisconsin DNR ArcGIS MapServer

DNR-managed properties including wildlife areas, state forests, and parks. Broader than SNAs, these include actively managed game and recreation lands that nonetheless provide significant habitat value.

⚠️ WI DNR PFAS Contamination Sites

Source: Wisconsin DNR PFAS Surface Water & Fish Data MapServer

PFAS ("forever chemical") detection sites in surface water and fish tissue across Wisconsin. The alerts engine flags any PFAS site within 1 km of a waystation or corridor site — contaminated water sources can harm pollinators that drink from them, and plants in contaminated soil may be affected. Displayed as red warning circles.

🌿 NLCD 2021 — National Land Cover Database

Source: MRLC / USGS National Land Cover Database 2021, served via MRLC GeoServer WMS

The 30-meter resolution national land cover classification for 2021, with 16 classes covering water, developed land, forest, shrubland, grassland, agriculture, and wetlands. Each of the 16 classes is available as a separately toggleable layer, served through a custom Node.js PNG pixel-filter proxy that strips all non-matching pixels to transparent. This makes it possible to isolate exactly one cover type at a time. The 🐝 Bee-Crop Field Fringe heatmap is also derived from the NLCD Agricultural classes (codes 81 and 82).

🌾 CDL 2023 — USDA NASS Cropland Data Layer

Source: USDA NASS CropScape WMS

The full-color USDA crop-type raster for 2023, showing individual crop species mapped at 30-meter resolution. The CDL statistics endpoint also provides county-level acreage breakdowns used to compute the Pollinator Mismatch alert — comparing bee-dependent crop acreage (alfalfa, cranberries, cucurbits, apples, etc.) against habitat site density to identify zones where adding habitat has the greatest economic leverage for crop yields.

🐝 USDA NASS QuickStats

Source: USDA NASS QuickStats API (requires free API key)

County-level agricultural census data providing Wisconsin managed honey bee colony counts and Brown County crop area harvested by species. When a NASS API key is configured (via nass-key.txt or NASS_API_KEY environment variable), this data enriches the Pollinator Mismatch alert with specific colony figures and Census-verified crop acreage, making the alert more actionable.

📸 Wikimedia Commons Photos

Source: Wikimedia Commons API

Georeferenced pollinator and habitat photos sourced via keyword search (bee, bees, butterfly, moth, pollinator, etc.) with client-side proximity filtering to the 15 km analysis radius. Photos appear as camera marker icons and open in a lightbox with attribution, license, and a link to the original Commons page.

🌳 WI DNR Urban Tree Canopy

Source: Wisconsin DNR Urban Forestry ArcGIS ImageServer

1-meter resolution tree canopy classification derived from NAIP aerial imagery for three survey years: 2013, 2020, and 2022. Each pixel is classified as tree canopy or non-canopy. Displayed as a raster overlay in the Land Cover & Cropland panel; the active year tracks the timeline scrubber. Canopy coverage % (within 150 m of each corridor site centroid) is also computed server-side via /api/canopy-check and shown in the site detail drawer. Note: WI DNR advises against direct change-detection comparisons between survey years due to methodology differences.

How the Intelligence Alerts Work

The alerts engine (alerts.js) runs entirely client-side against data already in memory — no additional network requests. Alerts are recalculated every time new data loads. Each alert type uses a specific spatial algorithm:

  • PFAS Near Habitat — haversine distance check, 1 km radius from each PFAS site to all corridor sites and waystations
  • Unsupported Sites — for each habitat site, checks whether any pollinator sighting exists within 500 m
  • Opportunity Zones — grid-buckets sightings at ~1 km resolution, flags buckets with ≥5 sightings and no habitat site within 800 m
  • Declining Public Zone — for each opportunity zone showing a sighting decline (>30% drop, prior year vs. current year) that also has a City or County parcel within 800 m; escalates to a Warning alert
  • Public Land Gap — City/County parcels ≥0.5 ac within opportunity zones with no habitat program site within 500 m; flagged as Warning (actionable without private negotiation)
  • Connected Pairs — finds habitat site pairs within 300 m of each other (ideal corridor stepping-stone distance)
  • Isolated Habitat — flags sites with no other habitat within 2 km (habitat islands that pollinators can't reach from the broader network)
  • Weak Nodes — corridor sites whose nearest corridor neighbour is 700 m–2 km away (beyond comfortable solitary bee foraging range, not yet fully isolated)
  • Connectivity Gap — finds the widest edge in the Minimum Spanning Tree over all corridor sites; flags if >2.5 km (exceeds most native bee foraging range)
  • Quadrant Coverage — divides the map area into four quadrants around the centroid of all sites; flags any quadrant with no habitat
  • Pollinator Mismatch — compares bee-dependent crop acreage % against estimated habitat coverage based on site count and assumed coverage per site
  • High Pesticide Pressure — flags corridor sites and waystations in counties with Critical-band pesticide intensity (band 4 of 4)
  • Poor Nesting Habitat — fires for corridor sites scoring below 25/100 on the NLCD-derived nesting suitability index (300 m radius, classes 31/52/71)
  • Shaded Habitat — fires for corridor sites with >55% tree canopy coverage within 150 m (WI DNR UTC 2022). High canopy closure can suppress the sun-dependent pollinator wildflowers that NE Wisconsin corridor sites are designed to support.

🧪 Pesticide Pressure

Source: Embedded county-level estimates derived from USDA NASS crop data and known pesticide-use patterns for NE Wisconsin

County-level agricultural pesticide intensity for the 9 counties in the Green Bay analysis region. Scores reflect crop mix (row crops, alfalfa, cranberries) and associated neonicotinoid seed treatment use. Displayed as a choropleth (pale yellow → deep red) with four bands: Low, Moderate, High, Critical. On by default. The Pesticide Pressure alert fires for any habitat site in a Critical-band county.

🪨 NLCD Nesting Suitability

Source: MRLC NLCD 2021, computed server-side via /api/nlcd-nesting

For each corridor site, fetches NLCD 2021 WMS tiles at zoom 13 (≈14 m/px) and counts pixels of three nesting-relevant classes within 300 m: class 31 Barren Land (weight 3), class 52 Shrub/Scrub (weight 2), class 71 Grassland/Herbaceous (weight 3). The weighted pixel fraction is normalized to a 0–100 score. Gray = Low (0–33, alert threshold <25), Tan = Moderate (34–66), Dark brown = Good (67–100). Scores are fetched asynchronously after corridor data loads and appear as badges on corridor site markers whenever any NLCD layer is active. The score and a plain-language description also appear in the site detail drawer. Tile results are cached 24 h server-side.

Technical Architecture

Bay Hive runs entirely in the browser as ES modules, served by a minimal Node.js static file server (serve.js) that also acts as a CORS proxy for data sources that don't send cross-origin headers. No build step, no framework, no npm install required.

  • Map rendering: MapLibre GL JS 3.6.2
  • NLCD tile filtering & nesting scoring: Pure Node.js PNG decoder — CRC32, Paeth predictor, palette PNG support, RGBA encoder — all without npm dependencies. The same infrastructure powers both the per-class tile proxy and the /api/nlcd-nesting batch scoring endpoint.
  • Caching: Browser IndexedDB (via a lightweight wrapper in cache.js); NLCD tiles cached 24 h server-side in memory
  • No tracking, no analytics, no external JS beyond MapLibre
